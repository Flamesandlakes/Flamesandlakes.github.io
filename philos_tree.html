A Network of Philosophers?

I will be the first to admit that I do not know a lot about philosophy. But I see an opportunity to learn something about, if not philosophy itself, then how philosophers throughout human history are connected and can be traced. This is a challenge to dive into a field where I have limited domain knowledge, as I seek to satiate my curiosity.

What I want to know in this case is how are philosophers and thinkers connected to each other. To answer this, I go through philosophers, listed from A to Z on Wikipedia, and collect information on who their influences are and who they themselves have influenced. I use the MediaWiki API to query the data.

To start with, this provides me withe 1761 unique pages listed from A to Z. There will likely be some selection bias in terms of what historical figures has a full-fledged Wikipedia article, which I do acknowledge. As I expand my search to influences listed on each of these pages, this increases to 4796 unique pages. Between them I count 9872 unique connections in total. On average, a node is connected to 2.31 other nodes.

These numbers covers the size of the data, but I would like to know more about the philosophers and how they are connected.

I begin with a directed network based on influence.

The nodes of the various philosophers and thinkers in the directed network is scaled based on degree, that is how many other nodes they are connected to in total.
The green gradient reflects the out degree, which is specifically how many other philosophers in the network that the philosopher in question have been a source of inspiration or influence.

The directed network above provides a decent overview. However, if I cheat and take out the connections among thinkers that are contenporary to each other, I get an acyclic network. All connections can be traced backwards until we reach their respective roots.

The root represent a philosopher or thinker with no ancestors of their own. This network contains 344 such roots from which we can trace the ancestries of all nodes.
Based upon similarities in root ancestries and lack thereof between nodes, I can separate nodes into different clusters. To be brief, this results in 7 groups, or clusters, derived from K-means clustering. These clusters can be used for color-coding nodes depending on what cluster they belong to.

As we note in the networks, there are some thinkers that appear to be prominent. I present a subset of these in the table below, which shows the 44 philosophers and thinkers that have the highest out degree, i.e. direct influence.

However after having removed common, less meaningful words also called stopwords, I still have more than 21000 unique words represented in the word embedding model. To keep things digestible, I will focus on the surnames of the 44 persons already highlighted.
